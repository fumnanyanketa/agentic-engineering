🎯 Objective: Learn to build an orchestrator-worker system and learn the judgment to know when not to build one. By the end you should be able to look at a task and say, with reasons, "this is a job for many agents" or "this is a job for one."

First, the words. A multi-agent system means you use more than one separate agent (an agent is an LLM, a large language model, running in a loop and using tools) and you let them split the work. The most common shape is the orchestrator-worker pattern. One agent, the orchestrator (also called the lead), breaks a big task into smaller pieces. It then hands each piece to a worker (also called a subagent), which is just another agent running on its own. Each worker does its piece and sends back a short summary. The lead reads all the summaries and writes the final answer.

This sounds powerful, and sometimes it is. But it is also one of the most over-used ideas in the field. Two well-known engineering teams have publicly disagreed about it, and the most useful thing you can learn here is not "which side is right" but "where the line between them sits." So we will lay out both cases honestly, then reconcile them.

The case for multi-agent

One AI lab built a research system in the orchestrator-worker shape: a strong, capable model as the lead, and several faster, cheaper models as the workers, each searching the web in parallel. On the lab's own internal test, this multi-agent setup beat a single agent (using the same strong model alone) by about 90 percent. The reported reason is that the workers run in parallel (at the same time), so the system can chase many independent leads at once. The team's own summary is that multi-agent "excels for breadth-first queries that pursue multiple independent directions simultaneously." A breadth-first task is one where you need to look in many places at once rather than follow one chain of steps. Example: "find every board member across all the technology companies in a stock index." That splits cleanly into many separate lookups. (a walkthrough of how this multi-agent research system was built)

The case against multi-agent

Another engineering team published a piece arguing the opposite, titled "Don't Build Multi-Agents." Their point is that parallel multi-agent setups are fragile (they break easily), because workers running side by side cannot see each other's work. Each worker makes small decisions, and those decisions quietly conflict. Their now-famous example: ask two workers to build a simple game, and one builds a background in one art style while the other builds a character in a totally different style, because neither knew what the other was doing. The result does not fit together.

From that they draw two rules:

  • Share full context, not just messages. If you do use helpers, give them the whole history of what has happened so far, not a one-line instruction. Most failures come from a worker missing context the others had.
  • Actions carry hidden decisions, and conflicting decisions produce bad results. Every step an agent takes commits to something (a name, a format, an approach). When two agents commit to clashing things, merging their output is a mess.

Their default recommendation is simple: "just use a single-threaded linear agent," meaning one agent doing one step after another, with one continuous memory. When that one agent's memory (its context window, the limited amount of text it can hold at once) starts to overflow, they add a small extra model whose only job is to compress the history into a shorter summary. (Don't Build Multi-Agents · a practitioner walkthrough of the argument)

Reconciling the two: the real boundary

Here is the part most people miss. The two teams do not actually contradict each other. They are describing two different kinds of work. The dividing line is read-heavy and parallelizable work versus write-heavy and dependent work.

  • Read-heavy and parallelizable means gathering information from many independent places. Nothing one worker finds changes what another worker should do. This is where multi-agent shines.
  • Write-heavy and dependent means each step changes the state of things, and later steps depend on the exact earlier ones. Editing a codebase is the classic case: step three only makes sense given exactly what step two did.

Notice that the pro-multi-agent lab itself admits the same boundary. In its own write-up it says that "domains that require all agents to share the same context or involve many dependencies between agents are not a good fit" for multi-agent work. That is, word for word, the other team's whole argument. So the honest takeaway is a rule of thumb, not a winner:

  • Multi-agent tends to win for research, broad search, and information gathering: many independent subtasks, often more information than fits in one context window.
  • Single-agent tends to win for sequential, dependent work like editing code, where each action changes the state and later steps lean on exact earlier decisions.

The cost reality

There is a price tag that the headline numbers hide. Running an agent (one LLM in a loop with tools) already burns far more tokens than a plain chat, because the model re-reads its growing history on every step. A rough figure from the research write-up is that a single agent uses on the order of 4 times the tokens of a chat, and a multi-agent system uses on the order of 15 times. (A token is a small chunk of text, and you pay per token, so 15 times the tokens means roughly 15 times the cost.)

That changes how you read the 90 percent number. It was measured on the team's own internal test, not an independent public benchmark, and it came at that much higher cost. The same team is clear that multi-agent only pays off "where the value of the task is high enough" to justify the spend. So always hold the two numbers together: the quality gain and the cost multiplier. A 90 percent quality gain that costs 15 times more is a good deal for a high-value research report and a terrible deal for a routine lookup. How to build the orchestrator-worker pattern (vendor-neutral)

The pattern does not need any special platform. In plain steps:

  • The lead agent reads the task and decomposes it (breaks it into smaller, independent subtasks).
  • For each subtask, the lead starts a worker agent in its own separate context, so workers do not crowd each other's memory. Give each worker enough shared context to avoid the conflicting-decisions trap above.
  • Each worker does its subtask (often by searching or retrieving) and returns a short, distilled summary rather than its full transcript.
  • The lead collects the summaries and synthesizes them into one coherent answer.

Any agent toolkit can express this, and you can also write it yourself with a normal programming loop and direct model calls. The pattern is the durable idea, not the tool.

Lab 10: Build an orchestrator-worker research system. The lead agent takes a research question, splits it into several independent subquestions, and dispatches one worker per subquestion (each in its own context, each using your tools or retrieval from earlier modules). Each worker returns a short summary, and the lead synthesizes them into one answer. Then build a single-agent version of the exact same task. Compare three things: quality of the answer, latency (how long you waited), and token cost. Write one paragraph on which one you would ship and why. The point is to feel the trade-off in your own numbers, not to pick a favorite.

✅ Best practices:

  • Reach for multi-agent only when the task is breadth-first, parallelizable, and high-value enough to justify the cost.
  • Give workers full shared context, not just a one-line instruction, so their decisions do not collide.
  • Always compare against the single-agent baseline on cost, not just on quality.

⛔ Pitfalls:

  • Using multi-agent by default because it "sounds sophisticated."
  • Quoting a quality gain while ignoring the roughly 15 times cost multiplier.
  • Parallelizing dependent work and then getting outputs that do not fit together.

🏁 Milestone: You have a working orchestrator-worker system and a reasoned, numbers-backed decision about whether it actually beats the single-agent baseline for your task.
